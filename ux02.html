Spotify Usability Evaluation

  • Dec,2015
  • Usability Study

Project Info

Spotify is a music streaming service that provides users with access to millions of songs. Up to Aug 2016, Spotify has garnered 39 million active subscribers worldwide.

Our HCI graduate team* performed a usability testting to evaluate Spotify’s desktop and iOS mobile app. The testing method hybrids cognitive walkthough and heuristic evaluation. Based on the evaluation results, we propose design recommendations for spotify desktop and iOS versions.


*Team Members: Shanglei Z., James M., Yhareli C., Rehab T. and Nitya R.

Overview

Spotify

Spotify is a music streaming service that allows you to listen to millions of songs from a large variety of artists and albums. Some of its features include: creating and sharing a playlist, following your friends, enabling collaboration on playlists to share them with friends.

Although Spotify is a great product, there is room for improvement. Our team identified some existing issues and has suggested recommendations for Spotify that could make it more intuitive and give music lovers a rewarding experience, both in desktop and mobile application.

Evaluation Summary

Before the usability test, we performed a qucik expert review to identify potential issues of spotify, and based on the findings we generated seven tasks, such as Search Songs, Create a Playlist, Add/Delete songs from a playlist, Follow Friends, Share Playlist, and Create Collaborative Playlist.

Usability Testing Tasks


10 participants were recruited to complete those tasks on both the web and mobile application. All users completed a post-task comfort rating as well as pre-test and post-test questionnaire to collect demographical data and assess the overall usability of Spotify (SUS score). After performing a competitive analysis between the Spotify Desktop and Mobile application we generalized results between both platforms.

To ensure the efficiency of our usability test, we conducted a pilot study before the full-scale test.

Overall, it was noted that the users could perform most of the tasks in the desktop application more easily as compared to the mobile application. Though users found the mobile application difficult to use in comparison with the desktop application, they found that the feedback from the mobile application very useful. Our final conclusions point to the Spotify mobile application being more cumbersome to use as compared to the desktop application.

Methodology

What We Tested?

Our team did a competitive comparison of the Spotify desktop application versus the mobile application. For mobile, we primarily focused on the iOS platform. We also performed the entire test using the free version.

Who We Tested?

A total of 10 users participated in the study. All users tested the desktop application as well as the mobile application. Users were from a variety of backgrounds that included undergraduate students, graduate students, and working professionals.

Where We Tested?

The majority of sessions took place in the study/conference rooms available in the library and the UITS lab. Other testing sessions took place at the participant's house or place of employment.

When We Tested?

User testing took place from November 9 through November 21, 2015.

How We Tested?

The format used was one-on-one usability test sessions. All sessions took around 60 minutes or less to complete.

Experimental Setup

For all user testing, an interviewer and observer were present. The observer gathered notes on the user’s reactions, facial and verbal expressions, etc. All users were asked to implement the Think-Aloud strategy throughout the session to provide further information. The interviewer clarified the tasks that were given to the user, guide them through tasks if necessary, and prompt users to think-aloud when they were stumped. Live video recording of all user-testing sessions was also taken to provide the team with a holistic view of user actions.

The following equipment was used for all desktop user-testing sessions:

  • iOS based laptop/computer with the Spotify Desktop Application installed on it.
  • AZ Screen Recorder, QuickTime Player and Captivate 9 for audio and video recording.

The list below consists of equipment used for mobile testing sessions:

  • Android / Apple phone with Spotify mobile application installed on it.
  • AZ Screen Recorder for audio and Video recording.

Users were asked to complete various tasks such as searching for songs, creating a playlist, adding songs to that playlist, finding friends and following them, sharing playlist with friends etc. A complete list of all tasks used for the mobile and desktop version of Spotify can be found in the appendix. In addition, users were asked to fill out a consent form and also complete a post-task and a post-test questionnaire. These forms can be found in the appendix of the report.

Performance Results

Summary of Results

After each task, participants were asked to rate the ease of completing the task on a scale of 1-5 (easy to hard). Participants considered that creating a playlist, deleting songs, and following friends harder on mobile compared to desktop.

  • TASK SUCCESS Users had higher success rate on desktop
  • TIME-ON-TASK Users succeeded quicker on desktop
  • ERRORS Users had fewer task errors on desktop
  • LEARNABILITY Users completed tasks on first attempt using desktop
  • SUS Users still preferred mobile over desktop

Ease of Use for Tasks Performed

After each task, participants were asked to rate the ease of completing the task on a scale of 1-5 (easy to hard). Participants considered creating a playlist, deleting songs, and following friends harder on mobile compared to desktop.

Level of Success

Generally, participants were more successful on the desktop than mobile due to faster access to additional options, fewer steps to create a playlist, and identifiable icons.

System Usability Scale (SUS Rating)

Participants answered a questionnaire consisting of 10 statements from a scale of 1–5 (Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree). Overall, the usability of the mobile app was slightly better than the desktop version.

Findings

Global results: Participants are more aware of what is happening (visibility of system status) better on mobile compared to desktop. There’s almost immediate feedback when the user performs an action on mobile but it’s almost non-existent on desktop. However, desktop has more prominent buttons and labels that allows the user to notice them quicker. Certain icons were not easily recognized by the user or were quickly dismissed. Users with a Facebook account are able to connect and perform certain tasks faster, such as finding friends, following, and sharing playlist.

Recommendation: For the mobile version we suggest a tab menu rather than a hamburger menu so that the user can rely on recognition rather than recall. In addition, visibility could be enhanced by increasing the color contrast between type color and background (e.g. participants did not notice when a playlist was collaborative even though the lable appeared above it).

TASK 1

Search Songs

  • Users were confused when the search results displayed songs, albums, and playlist with similar names, or only showed the artist that covered the song, instead of the original composer.
  • Users could not immediately identify that certain songs/artist were not not available.
  • Readability became an issue due to the low contrast between the background and type color.

Recommendation: Add Playlist as a main menu item on the mobile version.

TASK 2

Create a Playlist

  • Only 44% of the participants on mobile found the overall experience of creating a playlist easy compared to 72% on desktop. There are multiple steps required to create a playlist option on mobile, unlike the desktop version that has a visible "New Playlist" button on the left menu.
  • Users who tested the desktop version first followed by mobile felt there was no consistency or continuity between both version of the menu items.

Recommendation: Add Playlist as a main menu item on the mobile version.

TASK 3

Add/Delete Songs from a Playlist

  • On desktop, participants found it confusing that the option to “remove from playlist” is not grouped with the “remove” option. However, the “copy...” or “go to...” features are grouped together.
  • On desktop, the “Save to your music” (+) icon on/off feature was not clear to participants. Users mistakenly clicked on it thinking it would add the song to their playlist. Then it would change to a check mark and the users were unclear as to what this indicated.
  • As for the mobile application, users relied on the kebab menu to find the “remove from playlist” option, which was nested under two other options.

Recommendation: For desktop, improve error prevention by grouping “remove from playlist” with "remove from your music". For mobile, provide a direct option to remove a song from playlist.

TASK 4

Follow friends

  • 9 of 10 users found it relatively easy to follow friends after overcoming the challenge of finding friends.
  • It seems that Spotify gives preference to users with Facebook accounts when searching for friends. Non Facebook users had a harder time searching for friends.

Recommendation: Additional placement for Find Friend button.

TASK 5

Share a Playlist

  • Only 33% of participants on mobile, and 54% on desktop, found the overall experience of a sharing a playlist easy. Spotify seems to give preference to Facebook accounts when attempting to share a playlist. Non-Facebook users have a hard getting acknowledgement of shared playlist.
  • For mobile, users prefer to send links via a separate apps like WhatsApp or Messenger instead of Spotify. With this method, participants were able to verify faster if the other person received a link of the shared played. Conversely, less frequent Spotify users did not pay attention to the notification icon, which alerted that a playlist had been shared with them, and were unsure of it's purpose. Some found the push notification slow and hard to understand.

Recommendation: Visual cue for shared playlist: On mobile, adding a “SHARED” label using Spotify’s blue hue below the track’s name is a subtle but clear identification that the playlist is shared.

TASK 6

Create a collaborative playlist

  • On mobile, participants did not notice a visual cue and did not receive any feedback to indicate that a playlist is collaborative.
  • On desktop, participants considered the visual cue limited and almost dismissible due to the size of the circle and the low contrast between the medium-gray text color and the dark-grey background..

Recommendation: For desktop, improve error prevention by grouping “remove from playlist” with "remove from your music". For mobile, provide a direct option to remove a song from playlist.

TASK 7

Add/Delete Songs from a Collaborative Playlist

  • On mobile, deleting a song from the collaborative playlist has too many steps involved, hence it is difficult for the user to find how to delete a song.
  • User expected to find an option that removes song from the playlist when he/she clicks on the kebab against the song.

Recommendation: On mobile, provide an option to remove song from playlist on clicking the kebab.
