Huddle Mobile Application

  • April,2016

Project Info

Huddle is a facility booking application for IUPUI student/faculties. Integrated into the ONE.IU platform, Huddle makes meeting/conference room booking easy.

The application displays bookable meeting/conference rooms across IUPUI campus. Availability of rooms is color coded on the map. The user could quickly apply search/check/book actions via the app.

Unserstanding the Users through Contextural Inquiry


College students always have to meet as groups to discuss team projects. As IUPUI students, the University Library(UL) is the most popular location for group meetings, however, it's not an easy task to book a room in the University Library(UL) due to various reasons. To understand those reasons and to find a design solution, 12 Contextural Inquires were conducted with IUPUI students.

The 12 contextual inquiries were conducted respectively in the Graduate Lab in the School of Informatics, the lobby of Student Center, and a group study room in the University Library. We observed all of those group meetings to see how do they decide a place for the next meeting and how would they communicate before that meeting.

Through the contectural inqury, two main reasons were identified. First, the University Library is an independent system that does not even work with IU accounts, and this requires a user to register a UL account before using its facilities. Second, students have their preferred messaging apps, and usaually they are different. Those bring in communication barriers.

Interview and Observation Notes

Sensemaking 0f Data

User's Voices

Only 17% of participants feel familiar with UL booking system and use it very often. Many of them hadn't even heard about the booking system.

67% of participants use ONE.IU platform every day. They rely on ONE.IU to liaise with professors & classmates.

All participants use more than two instant message apps on a daily basis. Half of them have switch between 4-5 apps everyday to contact teammates, professors and family.

Only one-third of participants feel satisfied with current options for group meetings on campus.

What else we learned from the Contextual Inquiry

Beyound those quantified findings, I also gained the following insights:

  • Beside the library, sometimes students would also like to meet at on-campus coffee shop , online, or off-campus for group project meetings.
  • Students would like to tie their preferred messaging apps with the university platform, such as the ONE.IU.
  • Students would like an integrated system that allow them to check room availability, book and share the information with the team members.

Tangible, Rapid Solutions

Huddle App - A new extension of the ONE.IU Platform

ONE.IU is an existing platform that provides services and information at Indiana University. IUPUI students use it to register classes, check grades, pay tuition, etc., almost everything related to IU campus.

Similar to an app store experience, the design idea is to connect a third party messaging tools (user defined) & Huddle application to the ONE.IU platform, so that students could just use ONE.IU to check room availability, book room, share the information and communicate with the team members.

Evaluations

Evaluation Summary

In order to validate and assess our product, we conducted usability testing with four potential users. Our design solution to the problem of student communication and collaboration was three–fold. First, we allow for third party applications to be easily integrated with OUE. IU and our mobile app, Huddle. Second, we improve upon the existing OUE. IU to include features such as cross–platform communication and room booking features for campus buildings. Third, we take the first two solutions and make it mobile so that students can communicate and collaborate on the go.

Because the first two solutions were not as involved, we evaluated only the mobile application, Huddle, with potential users. All users were provided a Task Error Record Sheet, in which we asked them to note errors related to confusion, missteps, mistakes, task execution, and design and interaction problems while interacting with the prototype.

Evaluation Findings

Majority of the problems identified by the users are low severity. Issues are mostly related to terminology, messaging, calendar, and history.

Terminology

Some users were confused by some of the terminologies. For example, they did not understand the difference between “Available” and “Huddle” when reserving on-campus rooms for booking.

Messaging

One user did not understand the flow of the application after booking a room, because the app would jump over to the Messaging feature. He expressed that when a new message is received in the Messaging feature, that should be indicated in a red bubble instead of the existing blue color. Additionally, he felt that the app should take the user to the homepage instead of directing the users to the messaging feature immediately after booking a room. Additionally, some users felt that it 'd be good to include the names of individuals in each of the messaging group instead of an overall group label.

Calendar

Some users felt that the Calendar function of the app was too cluttered. For example, they expressed that there were too many “dot”s on the calendar, which were used as a legend to indicate which calendars the user can view at the same time, that ranged from Umail, Canvas, Huddle, and Gmail.

History

One user felt that the icon used to represent the History log needed to be changed. He expressed that the existing icon reminds him of a clock and thus, the icon should be improved upon to indicate History more clearly.


SUS Ratings

In addition to the Task Error Record Sheet, we had users complete a Post–Evaluation Questionnaire that we created. The survey comprised of 10 questions related to the System Usability Scale (SUS). Evaluators were asked to rate the system on a 5 point scale where 1 stood for “strongly disagree” and 5 stood for “strongly agree”.

Overall, the feedback that we received in the Post-Evaluation Questionnaire in terms of the utility of the product was very positive, but we attribute some of the lower ratings to the errors that evaluators mentioned during the Task Error assessment.


75% of the evaluators strongly agreed that they would use the system frequently.


100% of the evaluators either strongly disagreed or disagreed with the statement that they found the system too complex to use.


100% of the evaluators either strongly agreed or agreed with the statement that they felt the system was easy enough to use.


100% of the evaluators either strongly disagreed or disagreed with the statement that they would need help from a technical person to use the system.


100% of the evaluators either strongly agreed or agreed with the statement that the functions in the system were well integrated.


100% of the evaluators either strongly disagreed or disagreed with the statement that the system was very inconsistent.


100% of the evaluators strongly agreed with the statement that other users would be able to learn how to use the system fairly quickly.


75% of the evaluators either strongly disagreed or disagreed with the statement that the system was troubling to use.


100% of the evaluators either strongly agreed or agreed with the statement that they felt confident in their abilities to use the system.


100% of the evaluators either strongly disagreed or disagreed with the statement that they needed to learn a lot before being able to understand and comfortably use the system.
